Frequently Asked Questions

What is palm oil and what is the issue with it?

Palm oil is a vegetable oil that comes from the fruit of oil palm trees (Elaeis guineensis). It is found in about 50% of all supermarket products, from food to cleaning products, to skin/hair care and cosmetics. Oil palms are native to Africa but due to the increasing demand for palm oil, rainforests around the world, but particularliy in South East Asia are being cut down to make room for Palm oil plantations.

Today, Malaysia and Indonesia produce about 85% of the worlds palm oil, much of which is grown on the most biodiverse islands in the world, Sumatra and Borneo. These are the only islands where you can find animals such as the orangutangs, Pygmy Elephants, Sumatran Rhino's, Borneo Elephants, Proboscis Monkey, Borneo Bay Cat, and Sumatran tigers. They are just a couple of the animals going extinct due to habitat loss as a result of oil palm plantations.

Which animals are endangered as a direct result of palm oil?

Palm oil is listed as a main threat to 193 endangered species. Here are a couple of the critically endanged animals include:
Orangutangs:
Sumatran orangutan with less than 7,000 remaining
Tapanuli orangutan with around 800 individuals remaining
Bornean orangutan with around 100,000 remaining
Sumatran Tiger with less than 400 individuals remaining
Sumatran Rhino with less than 100 individuals remaining
Bornean elephant with less than 2,000 individuals remaining




How much land is being used for palm oil? How has this changed over the years?

There are about 19 million hectares or 47 million acres of land being used for palm oil production. This is 3 times more than when it was 6 million hectares or 15 million acres in 1990
