Palm oil is a vegetable oil derived from oil palm trees. These trees are native to Africa but are now being grown around the world, particularly in Southeast Asia. The rainforests of Indonesia and Malaysia are being burned, logged, and cleared to make way for palm oil plantations. Palm oil can be found in 50% of all packaged products we find in supermarkets, including food, hair care products, cosmetics, and cleaning supplies.

In 1978, palm oil was grown on 9 million acres, the vast majority in its native Africa. In 2018, that number increased to 47 million acres with the vast majority being grown in Southeast Asia, on land that was previously biodiverse forest. As palm oil demand continues to rise, more forest is being cleared to accommodate for palm oil plantations. The islands of Sumatra and Borneo are at the forefront of the deforestation. These biodiverse islands have created habitats for thousands of unique species including the last remaining orangutangs, pygmy elephants, Sumatran rhinoceroses, and Sumatran tigers. Palm oil plantations threaten the survival of these critically endangered species as well as another 189 threatened species that has palm oil production listed as one of their main threats.

The consequences of palm oil production have led to consumer boycotts. Some companies have bypassed the boycotts by not explicitly labeling ingredients as “palm-oil” leaving the ethical consumer unaware that the product contains palm oil. The European Union passed regulations so that European consumers are aware if palm oil is in a product. If the product contains palm oil, then it must be labeled as such. However, in the United States palm oil can be listed under 200+ different names. This site was created to help you determine whether a product is made with palm oil.
